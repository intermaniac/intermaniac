1962: John W. Tukey, an American mathematician, initially described the goal of data science. He predicted the inevitable rise of a new field about two decades before the first personal computers in his famous article "The Future of Data Analysis."

1962: Tukey was ahead of his time, but he was not the only one to have a keen interest in what would later be called "data science." Peter Naur, a Danish computer engineer, provided one of the first definitions of data science in his book Concise Survey of Computer Methods.

1977: The International Association for Statistical Computing (IASC) was founded with the goal of "linking traditional statistical methodology, modern computer technology, and the knowledge of domain experts to convert data into information and knowledge."

1980s & 1990s: The inaugural Knowledge Discovery in Databases (KDD) workshop and the establishment of the International Federation of Classification Societies marked a turning point in the development of data science (IFCS).

1990s: KDD, IFCS were among the first to place priority on the instruction and preparation of professionals in the theory and practices of data science. At this point, top experts looking to profit from big data and applied statistics began to pay more attention to data science.

1994: BusinessWeek wrote an article about the emerging practice of "Database Marketing." It highlighted how companies were gathering and using massive volumes of data to learn more about their clients, rivals, or advertising strategies.

1990s & onset of 2000s: Academic data science journals started to appear, and proponents of the field like Jeff Wu and William S. Cleveland continued to elaborate on the value and potential of data science.

2000s: Technology made huge strides, making internet connectivity, communication, and (obviously) data collection almost accessible to everyone.

2005: We now have big data. New technology equipped to process the massive volumes of data that tech behemoths like Google and Facebook were finding was required. Later, Spark and Cassandra made their debuts after Hadoop successfully met the challenge.

2014: Data scientists are in high demand across the globe as a result of the growing significance of data and enterprises need to identify patterns and improve business decisions.

2015:Data science now includes AI, DL, and ML. Over the past ten years, these technologies have propelled advancements in everything from tailored entertainment and shopping to self-driving cars, along with insights needed to successfully implement AI applications in our lives.
