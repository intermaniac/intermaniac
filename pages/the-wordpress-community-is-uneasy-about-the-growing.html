The WordPress community is uneasy about the growing pace of acquisitions. Every acquisition seems to foster a nervous dialog in the @post_status Slack. It's understandable: indie creators have been WP's backbone. ðŸ”¥ Hot take: The WP ecosytems *needs* roll-ups. ðŸ§µ ðŸ‘‡

First: what's a roll-up? Strategic acquisition of multiple WordPress businesses in order to create efficiens and economies of scale. It's a private equity model that's returning to popularity. Many small local biz like carwashes are in the midst of a roll-up golden age.

There's early signs of this thinking in the WP ecosystem. @AwesomeMotive has used its platform as an acquisition springboard for years. @stellarwp is clearly thinking consolidation. @jetpack has dabbled in acquisitions But the volume is still low given the ecosystem's size.

So what's the upside? FOR THE CONSUMER: WordPress' fragmentation makes it incredibly hard to get started. You're building a website for your small business: how do you know which of the umpteen form plugins or e-commerce integrations to use?

Consolidation helps create opinionated solutions that speak to the entirety of the customer's needs, not just the artificial feature divisions that define what most plugins do and don't do. Creating a website for your bakery? Here's everything you need. In one subscription.

I recently checked out @extendifyinc's onboarding experience. It's a fantastic model for what this should look like. Tell us what you're building for, and we'll tell you what you need. Build this, but with dozens of plugins to choose from under a single price tag.

Rollups, which spread costs like development and support across multiple products, can invest considerably more in the customer experience, too. 24x7 support with solid SLAs can kill a small biz owner. For a roll-up of 10s+ of plugins, it's tablestakes. Better for the customer.

FOR THE BUILDERS: Most WP plugin and theme businesses look like small SaaS startups. Small team with attention spread across dev, marketing, and support. It's a brutal life. Most never get past the first few hires, where your profit gets eaten up by each.

Many WP plugin and theme biz owners fight the trade-off between: - earning money but having no time, since they do /everything/ themselves â€“ having time and no money, since their payroll eats up their earnings. A roll-up creates liquidity for those biz owners.

And, as a strategic buyer, a rollup is well positioned to pay more for each acquisition. The purchase's economics need to work as part of an overarching vision, not just as a singular business.

If WP is going to continue to grow, the ecosystem needs to decrease its fragmentation and improve its customer experience. Consolidation is the clear path. And it isn't farfetched. WP is a massive economy. Lots of investor $$ will gladly take a piece of that pie.

WP serves multiple market segments: prosumers, SMB, enterprise. There's room for rollups (multiple!) across each of those market segments.

An industry insider that understands WP can execute this playbook in a way that's beneficial for the community: customers and builders alike. I hope someone takes up that mantle before an outsider gives it a try. The traditional "buy and cut costs" rollup model will be fatal.

What do you think? Agree or disagree, let me know your thoughts. If you're at #WCUS this week, I'd love to hear your thoughts, too.
