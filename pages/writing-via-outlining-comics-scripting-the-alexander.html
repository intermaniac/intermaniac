Writing via Outlining: Comics Scripting the Alexander Freed Way! a thread by Alexander Freed about scripting comics (Warning: Use for reference only. Not a recommendation.) https://t.co/c2OnJhBRMR

For me, a sequence is a dramatic unit that's all basically one idea, usually somewhere between 1 and 4 pages. If I've got a tense hostage negotiation that erupts into a fight, the negotiation and the fight might both be sequences even if they're all one "scene."

A montage of brief, related scenes is a sequence. An intricately choreographed combat scene might have multiple sequences. Try not to have a sequence shorter than a page--that's too granular at this stage, but it also suggests you'll run into structural difficulties.

(Like an issue, a chapter, or a quest, a page should have its own beginning, middle, and end. Cramming multiple sequences onto a single page is likely to conflict with this.)

Here's an example of sequences from the first chapter of my Kickstarting-later-today Violet Dawn comic (at https://t.co/T5giX8dDGz): 3 Pages: Fight 1 Page: Sad Aftermath 1 Page: Travel Montage 1 Page: City Reveal 4 Pages: Flashback 1 Page: Asleep

At this point, I can guess whether I'm over or under my page budget for an issue (if the sequences don't add up). If I'm wildly off, I probably need to rethink my issue structure. If I'm over or under by a page or three, that's fine--I just need to work on that next.

The next three steps have a default order for me, but often I'll rearrange them depending on where I expect to face challenges. My basic approach is: Break down each sequence by page. Break down each page by panel. Stub in rough dialogue for individual panels.

Breaking down a sequence by page is straightforward enough. Just keep in mind a page should feel like a miniature chapter in its sequence. My notes might look like this for a 3-page conversation: 1: Characters dance around the subject. 2: They argue. 3: End in frustration.

Breaking down a page by panel also doesn't need much explanation. I make brief notes on each panel, keeping in mind any special layout concerns. These are my notes for page 4 from the Violet Dawn comic:

1: Kaszek weeping. 2: Eyes, hears a whimper. 3: Kaszek approaches the injured hunter. 4: It hisses. 5: Kaszek feeds it. 6: It warms to him. (That's not exactly what ended up on the page, because @sollune knew better than to follow my script precisely.)

If you can't describe a panel in just a couple of words to yourself, you probably have too much going on for one panel. You'll want much more detail for the final script, of course, but a panel can do a single thing. One action. One emotion. Boiling it down helps keep me honest.

Stubbing in dialogue helps me make sure each panel can support whatever text I have in mind. Often I'll write a very rough draft of the final line--something that gets the gist across, even if it's clumsy.

Other times--if the exact line and line length doesn't matter much--I'll go even rougher, and just jot something like "Insert atmospheric exposition."

I try to keep a page to under 100-120 words total and fewer than 25 words per panel. There's lots of room past that but things start to look crammed. For a talky page, stubbed-in dialogue can be a big part of my process. For a quiet page, it's less important.

Remember how I said I sometimes do page, panel, and dialogue notes in different orders? For a dialogue-focused sequence, I might write a rough draft of a whole conversation before moving to panel beats based on emotion and character expression, then finally dividing by page.

For a complex action sequence, I might tackle panels first, then find the natural page breaks, then add stubbed text at the end.

The important thing is that the process gives me confidence that what I'm writing will fit with the structure and rhythm of a comic. Or if it DOESN'T, it means I can make modular adujstments--clip a page here, expand another sequence, shuffle panels around, etc.

By the time I've got rough notes for every page, panel, and line of text, I'm most of the way to an actual script. As I reformat the notes into something readable, add detailed panel descriptions for an artist, and polish dialogue, I also revise and edit and make more changes.

Once I finally have a "first draft" script, it's basically a second draft already. That means changes in the third pass tend to be minimal (but I do a third pass anyway). Of course, everything is subject to change once it gets to an artist, but that's a whole other discussion.
