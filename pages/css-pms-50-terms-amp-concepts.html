CSS/PMS: 50 Terms & Concepts You Must Know In this thread, I will be discussing 4/5 to terms and concepts a CSS/PMS aspirant must be aware of. However, it will be a brief introduction, hoping that you would study them thoroughly on the internet.

1. Praetorianism Derived from the Roman Praetorian Guard, who became increasingly influential in the appointment of Roman emperors, Praetorianism means excessive or abusive political influence of the Armed Forces in a country

According to Daniel R. Headrick, who popularised this term, praetorianism is a type of militarism that does not aspire to fight or win international wars, but instead to maintain its influence in the domestic political system.

Generally observed in developing countries, a praetorian army is interested in controlling decisions that could affect the interests of the military as a corporation or supporting some particular political faction or party.

2. The Garrison State First introduced in 1941 by Harold Lasswell, the Garrison State is a state dominated by the military-industrial complex. Lasswell was particularly influenced by the development of aerial warfare, especially as employed during the Second Sino-Japanese War

Harold Lasswell postulated that there is a possibility of a political-military elite composed of "specialists in violence" to dominate the policy formulation in a modern state.

Harold Lasswell's ideas were later employed by President Eisenhower who believed that a deep, even paranoid, fear of the military might and superiority of USSR would turn the USA into a "garrison state", with an economy dominated by military spending and civil liberties eroded.

The military-industrial complex became a force in itself, consuming a majority of American discretionary federal budget at the expense of infrastructure, educational or health spending, spreading fear among the populace and looking for enemies to replace USSR after Cold War.

3. Most Favoured Nation Clause Used in trade treaties Most-Favoured-nation Clause, simply means a country providing a trade concession to one trading partner must extend the same to all. Now also used in commercial law, to denote requirement of equal treatment for all customers

It has nothing to do with extending any extraordinary favours to any country seeking MFN status. That’s why In U.S. trade legislation, most-favoured-nation treatment is rightly described as "permanent normal trade relations" to avoid the implication it confers preferential status

Loss of MFN, which underpins WTO, status exposes a country to discriminatory import tariffs on its products. For example, if a country belonging to the WTO reduces or eliminates a tariff on a particular product for one trading partner, it must extend the same to all WTO members

4. Containment Articulated by George Kennan, US Ambassador to former USSR in an article written in Foreign Affairs (Article X), Containment was official US foreign policy doctrine, during Cold War on how the US should react to the expansion of international communist influence.

Initially, Containment meant the military defence of its allies in Western Europe. However, it later became a more aggressive policy when the Americans invested military support in protecting South Vietnam from internal and external communist pressure.

5. Détente During the 1960s and till the fall of the former USSR, the word ‘Détente’ remained popular in political vocabulary. It signified a foreign policy process mainly concerned with an easing of tension between the former USSR and the USA.

Now, any policy which involves self-interested economic co-operation, or steps towards reduction in the level of armaments, is likely to qualify as an example of détente.
