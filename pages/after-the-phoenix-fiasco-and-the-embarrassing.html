After the Phoenix Fiasco and the embarrassing reversal of the https://t.co/V86zl8sSmj email change, I sometimes wonder: what if the Canadian federal government just developed its own operating system (#Linux) ? A thread 1/16 https://t.co/rAcrmPpYRj https://t.co/i0otfH7ONj

Developing dedicated software for government is not as crazy as it sounds: there are already multiple governments that don’t use Windows. This Wikipedia page lists governments that use Linux. 2/16 https://t.co/GJXVGeecms

There are a few European adopters, including Vienna and the French National Gendarmerie. But the most striking example is the US Department of Defense #DoD. 3/16 https://t.co/ZUjT7wPLK8

If public organizations from the US military and to Government of Andalusia can develop their own versions of Linux, surely Canada can too! But, “why,” one might ask? 4/16

The federal government has had several high-profile disastrous software episodes, including the Phoenix pay system (problems with IBM), and the adoption and reversal of a single email domain (problems with Microsoft). 5/16 https://t.co/i0otfGPFzb

In Canada, different federal departments also use different software; for example, agencies that use SharePoint can’t easily collaborate with those that use (Canada-based) OpenText. 6/16 https://t.co/BqzJQOXnkU

A single operating system (most practically based on Linux) could avoid a lot of these problems. Software could be developed in house, updates could be done at once (without the need for changing contracts)… 7/16

...and data could be stored entirely in Canada (protecting privacy), and economies of scale could be obtained by putting all departments on a single system. 8/16

An in-house operating system could have special features designed for government, such as quickly preserving data for Access to Information Requests (something tough to do with MS Office, and for which special software is needed). 9/16 https://t.co/4IJu9wSaxZ

Another example: complete integration of the staffing and payroll system into the OS (right now, Phoenix runs separately from Windows). The software could also be simplified, ditching features that government workers do not need or use (Groove Music, Xbox Game Bar, etc.) 10/16

It could also conceivably be offered to the provincial governments, as a cheaper and Canadian alternative to Windows; and even other countries (such as LICs that don't want to pay Microsoft's fees). 11/16 https://t.co/J55WwZ723f

(For the name, it could be something as bland as “GoC-OS”, or something more creative, like “Maple.” Too bad “Acer”, the Latin species name for Maple, is already taken. Or maybe “RedLeaf” Linux would be best.) 12/16

I admit I'm no software engineer and tweeting is easier than writing code. Perhaps the data security requirements for an operating system are best handled by a big conglomerate like Microsoft, instead of in-house in Canada. Then again, Linux is already used by other govs. 13/16

And though it would cost a fortune to develop and implement (plus the agony of training staff), in the long run, the transition would likely save money and allow for greater control and customization than is possible in the current system of outsourcing to American firms. 14/16

Canada once had a thriving high tech sector, with firms like Corel (@alludo), Nortel, and @BlackBerry at the cutting edge. Perhaps it is too late to recover that lost intellectual capital. 15/16 https://t.co/xOBMKVJ2xQ

Ottawa was "Silicon Valley North;" it could be again: a big Trudeau-like initiative like this could reinvigorate the Canadian software industry for the 21st century. 16/16 - END
