Learning is a lifelong human behavior. As I've mentioned before, I'm 55 and just learning how to "really" code. My hope is this thread makes others feel more comfortable and confident in their journey. I've never really had to use git, and it's been bothering me: a ðŸ§µ

First, hat tip to @yechielk who took time to both encourage me and talk me through some of the thought processes (the "why do I need this?!?") that sit behind basic git actions. And for those reading this and wondering what doesn't make sense, just know that...

For ops folks, git, CVS, github, etc. make ZERO sense. My first thought was "why not just have a folder for your app and include versions in the filename (i.e.: myapp_1-2-4.php)" Yes, I know why NOW. But starting out it was extremely opaque.

So anyway, I owe a debt of gratitude to @yechiel for being patient with my whiny ass. Lesson 1: if you find someone who will let you be cranky as you learn, that's gold. Recognize and value them.

So with his direction, I just started hacking through the basics using a fake github repo and stupid simple files (hello_world.py) type stuff. And eventually I *thought* I had the concepts, but didn't have anywhere to apply it.

But today was a major milestone. I'd been making a bunch of updates to the apps I wrote to calculate prayer times for my synagogue - both the daily and the full year calendars. (just trust me, this is not trivial).

But I'd gotten lazy. My tracking (filename_version format) was becoming sloppy. So I git cloned the external repos I had manually (through the GUI) created forever ago. Then I updated each of the (local) files to match production...

Then from the command line I git add-ed, git commit-ed, and git push-ed them. Could I have used the built-in tools in something like VSCode or @GitKraken ? Of course. And in the future of course I will.

But the point here is to learn, and (for me at least) doing it the "hard" way first makes using the labor saving tools make sense. Most importantly (to me) the entire process - while not muscle-memory simple - was understandable and I could make sense of what I was doing...

...both in the immediate and also the larger "this is how REAL programmers do it" contexts. And that felt like a really big deal.

Of course there's more to do. And I understand that "I can do git" does not a dev make. For example, I still need to practice branching. But now the "why" of that is becoming clearer, which means the how will be easier too.

That's it. Just me talking about a step in my journey. Making space to be bad at something, and then be less-bad-but-still-a-lot-to-learn at something. And eventually be good at something. Because that's how everything is. For everyone.
