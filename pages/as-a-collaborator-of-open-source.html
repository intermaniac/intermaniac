As a collaborator of open-source projects, I can say that most of my time is NOT spent on coding but answering users, code reviews, discussions, ... It happens mainly on GitHub, and it's easy to lose control of the notifications. Over the years, I got a flow that works for me.ðŸ§µ https://t.co/GhznGeQ642

I often get swamped, especially after a week at an event or some PTO. I've accepted as a fact of life that I'll have more than a thousand notifications on GitHub from time to time. It's also clear that I can address them without blankly marking everything as read. https://t.co/wpcyd0vtT0

I subscribe to ALL events for each project I deeply care about. How to do it? Go to the repository, click the "Watch" option, and select "All activity". This way, you will be able to see new issues and PRs, even if it means you'll get many irrelevant notifications. https://t.co/J6MqLu7PK3

For each project that is a critical dependency on the project I deeply care about, I subscribe to the "Releases" activity. https://t.co/sXAuaSdlku

At the beginning of my day, I open my notifications. I might have direct pings, updates to issues I opened, all activity from the projects I deeply care about, and new releases for essential dependencies. I start with the projects with fewer notifications.

While reviewing the notifications, I use the "2-minute rule" from GTD: if it takes me less than a few minutes to complete the task, I'll just do it. Sometimes, "completing the task" means delegating or pinging other folks for input.

At the end of this first cycle, I'll have cleared out all items NOT related to the projects I deeply care about. This gives me a warm sense of accomplishment. Next up, I might have hundreds of notifications for the projects I deeply care about.

Before I start using the 2-minute rule again, I select one of the projects and get only the "reason:subscribed" events. I wasn't pinged on those, I wasn't assigned, or asked to do a code review: those are potentially irrelevant to me. https://t.co/3rGyCLSwdq

I typically just read the title for those items and mark it as "done" if it seems irrelevant. If it's a subject that interests me, I leave it as is and go to the next one: I'll handle it later.

By now, all notifications I have are for items I care about: I was pinged, got assigned to work on it, asked to do a code review, or it's an item that seems otherwise relevant to me. Here, the 2-minute GTD rule applies again.

For all items that will take more than 2 minutes, I'll first add them to a to-do list app (@todoist is my current choice) and then mark them as "done". This way, I'll have a zero inbox at the end of the morning catch-up, with my complete task list residing elsewhere.

The final trick is this: I typically WON'T check my notifications for the rest of the day once I cleared my inbox. I might go over the same item multiple times during the morning catch-up, but once my inbox is empty, I will focus on my own tasks.

This is what works for me. With that, I can get from over a thousand items to only a few ones in a few days without having to "mark everything as read". In general, I don't miss important notifications either.
