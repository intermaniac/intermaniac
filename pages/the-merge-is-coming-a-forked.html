But, what could happen on the forked Proof of Work (PoW) chain? First of all, we need to define what a replay attack is. A replay attack is taking a transaction from one network and using it on another network. 3/

If the forked PoW Ethereum's chainId remains at 1 and does not change, replay attacks are possible. Replay attacks mean that any transaction broadcast to the forked PoW network can be replayed on the PoS network and vice versa if both networks share the same chainId. 4/

When it comes to the Maker Oracles system, the main issue is within the median.sol contract. In short, the median is the smart contract that provides Maker's trusted reference price. ‚Üì https://t.co/sw3vHZZX72 5/

It works by maintaining a whitelist of price feed contracts that are authorized to post price updates. Every time a new list of prices is received, the median of these is computed and used to update the stored value in the Maker Protocol. 6/

The median.sol contract does not have any verification of chainId value, meaning that collateral prices on the PoS chain can be used via replay attacks to ‚Äúforce‚Äù the Maker Protocol system to ‚Äúwork‚Äù on the forked PoW chain. 7/

Forcing oracle values coming from the PoS chain into the PoW chain would likely overvalue the underlying collateral of the Maker Protocol on the PoW chain, meaning that Dai on PoW would be backed by less value in reality than the system would think. 8/

It should be noted that, in this case, undercollateralized Dai on the forked PoW Ethereum network is *not* expected to impact Dai on PoS Ethereum. 9/
