25/ In the early days of #Linux, companies would have had their doubts about the safety of going with this open-source Unix operating system that anyone could contribute to.

26/ They were happy to pay #SunMicrosystems, #HP or #IBM for their proprietary Unix platforms which came with support contracts and hefty price tags that gave those organisations peace of mind.

30/ Although public blockchain networks are a different paradigm ‚Äî they are not just open source projects, but also real live networks, there is an inevitability that as they become more entrenched in our lives, more companies will start to contribute to their development.

31/ At the current time, it's mainly web3 companies such as @Consensys and the various teams who wrote the client node software for these networks. But as more banks, exchanges and infrastructure providers start to rely on them, they will start to invest more heavily in them.

32/ Like when we look back at the 30 plus years of #Linux, I'm sure by the time we're in the 2040s if not sooner, we'll see similar dominance by corporates as the significant contributors to #Ethereum and other public blockchain networks.

33/ Such a future really doesn't seem that different from what we have now, after all, core internet services such as #DNS are already widely used decentralised public internet services.

35/ Regulation does have a part to play in the governance of blockchain networks. After all, if a bank is to do anything with customer funds that touches public networks even if it's in a completely secure manner, there will need to be regulation that allows them to do this.

36/ This will take time to become established. However, if you want to consider what the likely end state of governance for public blockchain networks looks like, I do encourage you to consider what happened to #Linux.
