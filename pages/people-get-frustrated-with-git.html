People get frustrated with Git. I mean, *really* frustrated. Why? It's not because Git is bad. It does a great job of tracking your work history, of letting you experiment with new features, and of collaborating. It's reliable, and works on a many of platforms. A thread! ðŸ§µ

So, what's not to like? In computer science, one of the core ideas is that of *abstraction* -- that you don't need to know how something works in order to use it. You can ignore the low-level details, to concentrate on higher-level ideas.

When you drive your car, you don't (and can't) keep track of all the things happening inside. You treat your car as one object, not thousands of connected objects. In that way, you can drive. But you can also think at a higher level, talking about traffic (i.e., many cars).

That higher-level perspective is also crucial when building software systems. You can't possibly think about all of the things happening on your computer when a program is running. Fortunately, the OS provides an abstraction layer to your computer's inner workings.
