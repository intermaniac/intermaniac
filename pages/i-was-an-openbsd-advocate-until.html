I was an #OpenBSD advocate until I found #Gentoo #Linux, and now I have this to say I would be *very* suspicious of any application or operating system that thinks it needs pledge() and unveil() on top of the Unix security model. Thread:

pledge() works by denying applications access to syscalls they don't need for normal operation, while unveil() together with pledge() restricts an application's view of the file system.

But if you think an application has a chance of executing arbitrary, uncontrolled syscalls or breaking out of chroot or VM jail, that isn't something you cover up with cutesy mitigations. That's a fundamental bug in your application, OS or overall security model

pledge() and unveil() are hacks at best, and at worst are votes of no confidence in the underlying implementation of Unix primitives or in the Unix model itself.

Linux can fully support radically modern security models such as MAC or RBAC as well as containers and virtualization, and is well audited by a worldwide coalition of hobbyists and corporate backers, all with competing interests. Even #OpenBSD can say none of that.

TL;DR: pledge() and unveil() are an intended substitute for actually writing secure code. Please do not use BSD in 2022, and I apologize for any harm I may have caused by supporting it.

The only reason I used BSD was because it seemed like a less bloated Linux at the time, and I now know that Gentoo is a less bloated Linux that still retains all of Linux's advantages over other operating systems.

Developers: Either use #rustlang or do C/C++ right, don't rely on dangerous excuses like pledge() to stop attackers. Admins: Abandon BSD, especially #OpenBSD, immediately. It is clear that none of them have a concept of what #cybersecurity means in the 21st century.
