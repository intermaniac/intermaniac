Now, let's get to the ðŸ¥©. Knowing the causal structure underlying some observations is hard. In fact, in general, it is very hard. Much research has been done, and yet causality is a central topic of research in many programs, including epidemiology, economics, ML... 2/n https://t.co/lit8ynBn6n

Some time ago @eliewolfe and collaborators developed a general technique that relaxed the question "Can I produce these observations in this causal structure?" into a hierarchy of questions that were easier to answer. This was called Inflation. https://t.co/qDt4yvirV1 3/n

Inflation techniques had (and has) a huge impact in quantum information theory: they have powered the field of network nonlocality, and, for instance, allowed to demonstrate that genuinely multipartite correlations exist in nature! https://t.co/EJWnW3AptX 4/n

Soon we developed inflation-based ways of characterizing correlations in *quantum* causal scenarios. This is the technique of quantum inflation, about which I wrote some time ago https://t.co/L0bJYftWh8 5/n

Despite all the success, inflation techniques are difficult to implement in practice: you have to write large linear or semidefinite programs (and, before that, know what linear and semidefinite programming are), that differ for every causal scenario you want to study. 6/n https://t.co/kP8lzAo0Tw

This is what our library solves. Inflation is a library for using inflation methods without needing to care about the details! In its simplest form, you define your causal structure, your distribution, and Inflation returns whether they are compatible or not 7/n https://t.co/PfBBa1qFQG

We put special care in making very easy to do feasibility problems and extracting certificates of infeasibility (a.k.a. Bell-like inequalities), but also optimizing functionals and finding critical parameter values (like visibilities) 8/n https://t.co/YDmuFC7mla

Version 1.0.0 "only" implements method for quantum inflation, but you can use Inflation to run many other problems of interest in quantum information theory. For instance, you can characterize classical correlations with a simple turn of a flag. 9/n https://t.co/DLcuOyFPVd

You don't even need probability distributions: you can assess whether any distribution with a particular *support* can be generated in a classical or quantum causal structure. This is, you can find Hardy-type arguments of compatibility. 11/n https://t.co/pIskRu92EA
