üßµ 2/7. The "Eclipse of Darwinism" (a term coined by Julian Huxley) covers a period of roughly forty years after the death of Darwin in 1882. During that time, although #evolution was widely accepted, natural selection fell out of favour as the principal explanatory mechanism. https://t.co/0UETVHA1R2

üßµ 3/7. Although the work of many biologists from this period is not so well known today, some left a great mark on the field of evolutionary biology, and shaped how it developed. https://t.co/iN7n0Y90cR

üßµ 4/7. William Bateson (1861-1926). Coined "genetics" and "epistasis". A leading saltationist, he opposed evolution by gradual change and emphasised the importance of mutations. Along with De Vries he rediscovered Mendel's work on inheritance, and co-discovered genetic linkage. https://t.co/lqys26zWaY

üßµ 5/7. Ernst Haeckel (1834-1919). Zoologist, naturalist, and populariser of Darwin's work. Coined "ecology" and "phylogeny". Asserted the importance of embryology as proof of evolution, and proposed the idea that "ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny". https://t.co/d5dxzPckUh

üßµ 6/7. Hugo De Vries (1848-1935). Coined "mutation", and developed the mutation theory of evolution. Independently discovered Mendel's laws of genetic inheritance, and rediscovered his work with Bateson. Argued that evolution (esp. speciation) occurred by large-step mutations. https://t.co/VCoI0tt2TW

üßµ 7/7. August Weismann (1834-1914). Most well-known for his idea of "the continuity of the germ plasm", which fundamentally undermined Lamarckism. He proved that while changes in germ cells (gametes) affected somatic cells, this interaction was one-way. https://t.co/nvZ8Lvjke7
