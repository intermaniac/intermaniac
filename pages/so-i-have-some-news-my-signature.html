I started tinkering with CSS back in 2007. For a decade, I stumbled my way through it. I was able to get stuff done, but I wasn't confident. I had my go-to snippets, things I could copy/paste to solve specific problems, but sometimes they didn't work and I didn't know why. 😬

I decided that I was sick of struggling with CSS, and so I started actively trying to understand it. When I'd run into some funky behaviour, I'd settle into the problem like a warm bath, and work through it until I understood.

This took a few different forms. I'd read up on MDN. When that wasn't enough, I'd drill even deeper, to the CSSWG spec. And then I'd experiment, test my assumptions. If I remove a critical CSS property, do things break the way I expect?

It felt like doing a jigsaw puzzle, but without the box. I started with a bunch of loose pieces, colorful blobs. I had no idea what they depicted. But as more and more puzzle pieces started snapping into place, I started being able to see the koala hanging in the tree. 🐨 https://t.co/xuETqQgtpk

I kept having epiphanies, like “ohhh, THAT's why this happens!”. Instead of relying on memorized snippets, I started being able to leverage my intuition. And holy smokes, CSS is *so* much more fun now! A robust mental model for the language does wonders. 😅
