In Hindu tradition, the Moon is the Chalice of Immortality, the Ruler of the Ocean,...

In Hindu tradition, the Moon is the Chalice of Immortality, the Ruler of the Ocean, and the Lord of the Plants. On this lunar eclipse, we explore (in the thread below) the various depictions and characteristics of the Moon from the classical texts of Hinduism… https://t.co/jCrtg8bo3L

In the Mahabharata (one of the two foundational Sanskrit epics of ancient India), ‘Soma’ is the most frequently mentioned name for the Moon. Soma refers to the beverage of immortality (amṛta) consumed by the gods and first produced by the churning of the ocean. https://t.co/TfeNvNCbbC

It is the essence of life and is frequently identified with the Moon in many Hindu texts, including the Rig Veda, the Atharva Veda, and the Brāhmanas. The qualities of gentleness, coolness, and healing associated with the moon go hand in hand with the seraphic Soma beverage. https://t.co/9xd7h6Lo3i

This Soma is the ever-flowing, ceaseless drink of the divine. Just like the warm, nourishing glow of moonlight that falls in a sea of darkness and lightens the path all around, this ambrosia is a drink that removes the earthly dark and has the celestial glow fill our being. https://t.co/8hETZNKxpV

Soma is the chalice of immortality and thus does not belong to the realm of death. Its association with the Moon has resulted in the planetary body being a symbol of the world beyond death—the sphere where ancestors dwell and can never die. https://t.co/XKxTULXmVC

The Moon was also believed to be made of the bones of Kama, the God of Sexual Love and Lust, whose body was destroyed by the glance of Shiva, the great yogi and supreme being. For this reason, ‘Soma’ also refers to semen, to the essence of life, to sexual and creative energy. https://t.co/84tJH3PXgz

The Moon can thus be seen as the chalice of life, renewal, and immortality. Since in yogic tradition semen is seen to be of the same substance as the mind, the moon is regarded in many texts to be the mind of the Cosmic Man (Virāt Purusha), the presiding deity of the mind. https://t.co/kywU3y6M7N

The lunar eclipse is believed to occur when the Moon is full and the north lunar mode, named ‘Rāhu’, attempts to devour it. According to the story (from Vijayānanda Tripathī), the gods were receiving the ambrosia of immortality (Soma) when the Moon detected that Rāhu… https://t.co/37RgOM5FfS

…was present in the guise of a god. Despite the fact he exists only as a severed head (without a body), Rāhu could not be killed as he had tasted the ambrosia. Whenever the moon is full, Rāhu attempts to devour the Moon in revenge for this episode, which produces the eclipse. https://t.co/YKfvx82gqx

The Moon was also seen as the principle of the ‘primeval waters’ (Prasna Upanishad) and the presiding deity of the watery element, ruling the sea’s tides. The Moon’s sphere is the reservoir of rainwater and for that reason it is the Lord of Plants, protecting all vegetable life. https://t.co/Vf2NLmeWax

It is believed that medicinal herbs increase their activity during eclipses; that the Moon brings rain; that moonlight makes the lotus flower blossom; and that the Moon’s soft rays soothe the burnt Earth after the aggressive solar beams of the day. https://t.co/yCt860Ly90

The Moon is the sphere of the mind, separating the physical world (the Earth) and the transcendent world of truth (the Sun). It is the bridge, according to Tripāthī, that we must travel—from the material to the solar world of light and intellect. https://t.co/xjyoPWr6w7

The God of the Moon, Chandra (or Soma) is represented with a crescent on his forehead, all his attributes being white, and an antelope for a vehicle. He is also variously described as seated on a lotus, drawn on a chariot by ten horses, clad in gold ornaments, or holding a mace. https://t.co/LTPSXTovKG

Many epithets exist for the Moon in Hindu texts, including: Indu: The Drop [of Soma] Nishā-kara: Maker of Night Nakshatra-nātha: Lord of Constellations Shitamarīci: Having Cool Rays Kumudapati: Lord of the Lotus Shveta-vājin: Drawn by White Horses Shiva-shekhara: Crown of Shiva https://t.co/htaCWryaNW

Shiva was seen to wear a diadem with the crescent of the fifth-day Moon—symbolising life, sublimated eros, and the power of procreation. According to the Bṛhaddevatā, the moon’s principal name of ‘Chandramas’ is derived from the following expressions: https://t.co/VBvH2CXHKY

“The Moon ‘runs beautifully’, or ‘runs observingly’, or ‘runs as one worthy to be observed’; or ‘he drinks Soma’; or ‘he fashions the aggregate of beings’.” https://t.co/ZgjyEkiyns

In the Mahabharata, it is written that ‘when the ocean was churned, the Moon sprang forth joyfully, just after the goddess of fortune and prosperity (Lakshmi), spreading a cool light with its thousands of rays’. https://t.co/QSYREB3ZKE

Lastly, various texts give various parentage for the Moon: In the Vishnu Dharmottara and Skanda Purāna, the Moon is the son of Detachment (Atri) and his wife Benevolence (Anasūyā) In other texts, the Moon is the son of the Eternal Law (Dharma) or of the Resplendent (Prabhākara) https://t.co/TeQmhZUn9Q

Ultimately, in Hindu tradition the Moon is a symbol of gentleness, cool comfort, and the human mind. It nourishes plants, provides light in the darkness, and is a symbol of divinity and our godlike nature. When we imbibe the moonlight’s glow, we are made one with the Divine. https://t.co/apRArkjgTu
