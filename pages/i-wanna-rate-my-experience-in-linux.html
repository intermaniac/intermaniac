I wanna rate my experience in linux for the past couple of weeks, as an intermediate developer and I can kinda figure my way out of problems (because it’s a different rating for average users) in this thread: ( and I will add to it over time )

first of all, why use linux: I really don't give a fuck about most of the things that people say to advertise linux (privacy, open-source, etc...), and most people who advertise for these things don't even use them properly or don't contribute to them

I just like linux as a system, and I think it has so much potential held by some parts of the linux community ( the majority are great people but they don't speak up to weird people in the community ) and I think it's really good for development because it has so many good tools

however, I really hope that linux would get better over time and become as good or even better of an experience as other popular OSs, because the community is either too used to the flaws that they don't see them, or they're ignorants ( no offense )

second of all, choosing a distribution: A distribution is simply put, is a system built on top of linux, and includes stuff like: a package manager, for managing your apps and dependencies, a desktop environment of their choice, basically the GUI of the desktop, and many things

to make it super super simplified, it's basically another OS ( not actually but super simply put )

basically, I would recommend not getting too caught up in comparing distributions because most of the opinions don’t have your use case in mind, so just pick one that looks cool to you, and you're eventually gonna learn more about distributions along the way

couple of suggestions that could be good for beginners: - linux mint ( based on ubuntu I think ) - pop!_os ( based on ubuntu ) - manjaro (not arch linux) didn't try them all out, but I kinda recommend them because they're based on popular things so finding help online is easier

Hardware support: I’m gonna be completely honest, sometimes it’s really really good, sometimes it’s really lacking, it all depends on the drivers that were made, either by the community (which is super cool that the community makes effort to support things)…

Or from the manufacturer, and here lies an issue, most of the time, big billion dollars companies don’t give a fuck about supporting linux for the regular consumers, they only care about big corporations and shit, because it might not make financial sense for them…

Since the market share for linux isn’t that big, and sometimes rightfully not worth the effort. however, if it’s properly supported, it’s just gonna work really well out of the box, which is super nice
