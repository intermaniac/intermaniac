We just digitized dozens of vintage postcards of “Carnegie libraries" in Ontario! 🏛️ In the early 1900s, Andrew Carnegie funded 100+ libraries across the province, including in Toronto. Learn more in this thread. Or read our new blog post: https://t.co/yUoeee6kRK">https://t.co/yUoeee6kRK 🧵 1 of 8 https://t.co/aytXGV63Iy

Carnegie founded the Carnegie Steel Company — a huge steel manufacturer in the US eventually sold to J.P. Morgan for $480 million. 🏭 Though Carnegie became a great philanthropist, some criticized his practice of maximizing profits while paying workers low wages. 🧵 2 of 8 https://t.co/6Uj10dlHYP

Carnegie gave $56 million for 2,509 libraries globally, $2.5 million in Canada. 💰 For a grant, towns had to: 1. provide land 2. raise 10% of grant for annual upkeep Many in Ontario look similar as designs were all guided by Carnegie's secretary James Bertram. 🧵 3 of 8 https://t.co/yQwbwJJLt2

Most Carnegie libraries were built in the Beaux-Arts style, popular from 1880s to 1920s. 🎨 Their symmetry evokes formality & elegance. Greek and Roman decorative elements like columns, sculpted friezes, porticos & domes give the buildings a grand & imposing quality. 🧵 4 of 8 https://t.co/0dz5QClCop

Many Carnegie libraries were designed by local architects. Guelph architect William Austen Mahoney designed 15 of them. The libraries were often downtown, near parks, next to churches or town halls — like this one in St. Marys by local architect Joseph A. Humphis. 📍 🧵 5 of 8 https://t.co/EzjtJcQ4ZF

In Ontario, the first Carnegie library to open was in Chatham. It had a grand domed ceiling (something Carnegie’s secretary James Bertram thought wasteful). Collingwood had the first recorded request for an Ontario grant in 1899. Sadly, neither library remains. 🔥 🧵 6 of 8 https://t.co/4HZabuDsfD

Toronto got two grants. 💰💰 The first funded 4 buildings: Yorkville (1907); Central Library (1909), the largest Ontario Carnegie library (pictured); Queen & Lisgar (1909) and Riverdale (1910). A second grant built 3 more: Beaches, High Park and Wychwood (all 1916). 🧵 7 of 8 https://t.co/Zw8eKuv3sR

Nearby municipalities (now part of Toronto) also got Carnegie funds: Weston, Mimico and Western Branch in West Toronto (pictured). Many Carnegie libraries in Ontario still stand.
