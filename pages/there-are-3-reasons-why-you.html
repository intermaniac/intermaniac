Matching is a family of methods that aims at finding similar obs. in treated and untreated groups in order to compare them. The goal is to make causal estimates more reliable. (2/n)

To avoid the difficulties of matching in high dimensional data, researchers have proposed propensity score matching (PSM). PSM is matching on just one number - the probability of treatment (aka propensity score) - rather than on a full multidimensional feature vector. (3/n)

This sounds good on the surface, but there are three traps that await users of PSM: (4/n)

‚≠ï PSM is essentially a dimensionality reduction technique and as such it discards information. (4/n)

‚≠ï PSM can match observations that are very different just because their propensity scores are similar. This leads to estimating causal effects using two very different observations rather than two similar observations as we intended. (5/n)

‚≠ï **PSM paradox**. In the ideal (binary) case, propsensity scores for all observations should be close to 0.5. This means that the probability of being treated is really random for all units (a dream of any experiment designer!). (6/n)

[cont'd] ...Yet if all observations have propensity scores of 0.5 than matching becomes essentially random! This is known as **the PSM paradox**: the closer you get to the ideal, the further away you are from the ideal. (7/n)

What can we do in this case? üîµ One recommendation is to use distance matching or coarsened exact matching (CEM; https://t.co/qgHatUCmBt) instead (8/n)
