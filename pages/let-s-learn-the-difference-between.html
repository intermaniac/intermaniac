we have seen many types of units for specifying the length in CSS. For example absolute units: they are fixed i.e not dependent on anything like px relative units: they are dependent on other elements like the font-size of your browser like em, rem and vh

So rem first R stands for root in rem. It is relative to the font-size of the root or HTML element and in case it is not mentioned it takes the default font-size of your browser which is 16px mostly. So if your html has font-size = 16 px , 1 rem will be equal to 16px.

In the below example up to 400px width your HTML tag will have font-size of 10px and font-size of box which is 2rem will be 10*2=20px if you increase your width from 400px font-size of your HTML will be 30px resulting in 60px box font-size(30*2=60px). https://t.co/0MlWpxxHR7

on the other hand in case of em , its size depends on the font-size of nearest parent element, so if your parent tag has a font-size of 10px and you have font-size 2em , your effective font-size will be 20px(10*2 = 20px).

interestingly, while using em you can encounter an issue if you have many levels of nesting. in the example below for container we have font-size = 10px so for level 1 font-size will be 10*2=20px https://t.co/D4Pzla7UoK

But wait what for level 2. Its nearest parent is level 1 which has a font-size of 20px now. So for level 2 font-size will be 20*2=40px and similarly for level 3 font-size will be 40*2=80px. So even after having the same value for all levels, we are getting different font-size

Also, in case you give length in em to other properties like padding and margin, it will be relative to the font-size of the element itself. And in case you give length in rem to other properties like padding and margin, it will be relative to the font-size of the root element.

its good to use both em and rem combined in practice as each comes with its benefits and cons a good read for how to combine both and make the most of them https://t.co/uDLwxx4cHH
