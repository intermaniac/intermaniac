There are many forms of communication: - books - reports - articles - presentations - websites/blogs All of these come with their own file formats: pdf, docx, tex, epub, html, ... ðŸ™ˆ Juggling these formats is tricky. But things became much easier with Quarto. ðŸ§µ #quartoPub

What is Quarto? It's a publishing system that can bring together regular text and code to produce elegantly formatted output. More importantly, it can generate so many outputs from one single format (.qmd). Let's have a look at a few use cases. https://t.co/RGVr9eZJBp

1. Teach theory and application I'm teaching a probability class that involves math problems and R coding. With LaTeX (No. 1 math writing tool), it's a pain to mix texts, formulas and code. Not so with Quarto. That's why I'm to switching to Quarto (more on that tomorrow).

2. New journal Your paper was rejected at a specific journal. That sucks. What sucks even more is switching the journal template now. In Quarto, you can easily change the format to a different journal for a growing list of journals. https://t.co/yVzOuAg1Ao

3. Teaching material Many courses have their own website (because why wouldn't you make your courses accessible to all). But the online publishing format is different from, say, printed worksheets. With Quarto, you can generate the same teaching material from one file.

4. Share your wisdom on a blog You have an extensive knowledge in a scientific field? Maybe you want to share that knowledge and make it accessible online? With Quarto you can set up a blog or an online book in seconds and begin sharing your wisdom. https://t.co/Z5AJs8LjQa

Alright, enough use cases. How do we get started with Quarto? If you haven't programmed or used Markdown before, the easiest way to get started is to use the programming IDE RStudio. I think its visual editor makes Quarto feel like any other document. https://t.co/eRZteRPl2C

Note that Quarto does not depend on R (or RStudio). The beauty of Quarto is that it doesn't depend on a programming language. Thus, you can use it without any programming or with different programming languages But using RStudio is a good starting point https://t.co/T0FO986OqK

Once we have installed RStudio, we can use it to render Quarto files into different outputs. @minebocek walks you through the first steps in this video. She starts with an .Rmd-file (the predecessor of Quarto) but you can happily ignore this difference. https://t.co/Mphbd13jEU

For a longer intro, you can also watch the "Hello, Quarto" talk. https://t.co/fkNSqyXI2B

And for an even longer intro, there's an extensive 2-hour "Welcome to Quarto" webinar by @thomas_mock https://t.co/0mo9PiPn3S

So, where can you find more resources? First, there is the official Quarto "Getting Started" guide. The official Quarto docs are really great and I suggest you start there. https://t.co/jPcdiaiNEs

And then there is a whole range of blog posts, tutorials and presentations on Quarto. Thankfully, @MickaelCanouil curates them all at "Awesome Quarto". https://t.co/BzLVqE44Xn

Have fun exploring these options. I'll be back later today to show you a few more cool examples. In the meantime, you can let me know what you think about Quarto.
