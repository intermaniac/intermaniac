1/ Many folks ask us: "Bringing real estate on-chain is great and all, but how are you solving the oracle problem?" Let's explore what is the Oracle problem in crypto, how web2 experiences the same problem, and how we at Homebase are tackling it: A ðŸ§µðŸ‘‡

2/ First, what are blockchain oracles? Blockchain oracles are the means by which real-life data â€” like housing data â€” is transmitted to a blockchain. Imagine that Tim and Bob want to bet on the outcome of an F1 race. How would a smart contract know who to give the winnings to?

3/ Therein lies the oracle problem. How can off-chain data be securely reported on chain? On-chain data is only as reliable as the person inputting the data is. If you get faulty data on an immutable blockchain, smart contracts are executing decisions on incorrect inputs.

4/ Web3 critics forget that the oracle problem exists in web2 as well. Just the other week it was discovered that @Equifax supplied banks the incorrect credit score for millions of people! Imagine being denied a mortgage bc Equifax told the bank the wrong credit score ðŸ™ƒ

5/ There's already great examples of web3 companies tackling the oracle problem. @chainlink relies on an array of 3rd party partners to input information like stock or home prices. If a 3rd party inputs bad data, their reputation is on the line.

6/ Another company tackling the oracle problem in a good way is @BlockchainHomeR. They're relying on multiple 3rd parties (electric companies, water companies, etc) to input the relevant data for each home directly on-chain in exchange for getting access to homeowner data.

7/ At Homebase, data we input on-chain will be collected and verified by multiple 3rd parties. For example, we might partner with @HouseCanary to input housing prices on our platform or get inputs from different title companies before uploading liens of a home on-chain.

8/ The goal is to make real estate data publicly available and on-chain overtime as we scale. If real estate information / transactions were stored on-chain, we wouldn't need home title insurance (~$22B in savings) and home loans would be cheaper (~$35B in savings).
