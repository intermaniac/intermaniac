parametric models are frequently referred to as linear machine learning models. this is because the function's expected form is one that is linear.

the equation shows that the relationship between the input variables is linear. logistic regression and linear svm are two examples of parametric models in addition to linear regression.

non-parametric: non-parametric algorithms are those that do not rely on specific presumptions regarding the type of mapping function. the mapping function between the input and output data is not accepted as true by these algorithms in any particular form.

from the training data, they are free to select any functional form. as a result, parametric models need far more data than nonparametric ones in order to estimate the mapping function.

non-parametric may lead one to believe that there are no parameters. this is untrue, though. instead, it only indicates that the parameters are both adjustable and changeable.

the difference between parametric and non-parametric algorithms is thus crucial. we indicated that parametric algorithms, irrespective of the quantity of training data, have a set number of parameters.

the number of parameters for non-parametric models, however, depends on the volume of training data. the number of parameters increases with the amount of training data. this has the effect of making non-parametric algorithms' training times significantly longer.

an illustration of a non-parametric algorithm is k-nearest neighbours. for a fresh instance of data, it bases its predictions on the k training patterns that are the most similar.

notably, there is just one assumption made regarding the shape of the mapping function. it is presumable that training patterns with the greatest similarity will more likely result in a similar output.

we explained that one type of parametric model is the linear svm. this is due to the fact that fundamental svms are linear classifiers. non-parametric svms, on the other hand, are those that are not restricted by a predetermined number of parameters. parametric advantages:

simplicity. the processes used by parametric algorithms are simpler to comprehend. compared to non-parametric models, the results are also easier to interpret. data for training. compared to non-parametric algorithms, parametric algorithms require less training data.

fast training. they outperform non-parametric techniques in terms of computational speed. since they often have fewer parameters to train than non-parametric ones, they can be trained more quickly.

non-parametric advantages: performance. since non-parametric models offer a better fit to the data than parametric ones, they may provide predictions that are more accurate.

flexibility. these methods offer a strong fit for the data, as the aforementioned graphic demonstrates. they can fulfil a variety of functions.

very few, if any, assumptions. there are very few to no educated predictions concerning the mapping function. non-parametric algorithms get more knowledge from data than parametric ones do.

this is due to the possibility that the assumptions made by parametric algorithms may restrict learning. limitations of the parametric model: constraints on form. an algorithm must adhere to a predetermined functional form when using parametric approaches.

fit. the best fit to the data is not provided by these methods. they probably won't match the mapping function exactly. complexity. the complexity of parametric algorithms is modest. this indicates that simpler tasks are better suited for them.

non-parametric limitations: overfitting. these algorithms are more prone to overfitting even though they often fit data more accurately than parametric ones.

training data. these algorithms need a lot more information than parametric ones to provide an approximation of the mapping function. speed. non-parametric algorithms take longer to train since there are typically more parameters to take into account.
