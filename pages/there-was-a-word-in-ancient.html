There was a word in Ancient Greece used to describe those who, like the famous Oracle of Delphi, were thought to be possessed by a god: entheos. From it we get the word ‘enthusiasm’. (A short thread) 1/ https://t.co/nk9iJe5U36

To be ‘enthusiastic’ about something originally meant much more than being happy or excited; rather, it meant having your body be completely infused by it, as if hijacked by a divine parasite in search of a vehicle to unleash its powers. 2/ https://t.co/3qNKfxXBE5

Nietzsche knew this enthusiasm very well. When talking about Thus Spoke Zarathustra, he said Zarathustra first “came over him” as he was hiking, and that the first part of the book was conceived "with absolute certainty, as if every sentence were being called out” to him. 3/ https://t.co/TyfRpC7LDC

As a result (in aggregate) the whole book was put together in little more than a month. One month! 4/ https://t.co/4fYbCpK8qH

Instances of such divine emanation are plentiful (c.f. Da Vinci or Newton), and their consequences are terrifying and sublime in equal measure. Could it be that the greatest works in history have not so much been created as they have been discovered? 5/ https://t.co/tTpM7nEqpd

Or, better yet: that it was the works themselves who found their geniuses and used them to come to life, so that its artists were nothing but “a mere incarnation, a mere mouthpiece, a mere medium”—a mere necessity? 6/ https://t.co/sLMwTGEjFv

This (and more) is what I talk about in "Gardening a Self". You can keep reading with the link below: 7/ https://t.co/eO0F7H1Z5F
